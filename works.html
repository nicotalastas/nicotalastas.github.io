What actually keeps someone in a flooding house isn't stubbornness, it's fear of losing the one copy of
a birth certificate, of savings that took years to rebuild after the last flood, with no system telling them
how to prepare for that loss before the water starts rising. The barangay's only tool was a megaphone
and an evacuation order: high-stress, reactive, arriving after the decision to stay had already been
made. Nobody had built anything for the calm months before the emergency, so I did.

A household that's BaHanda doesn't just survive the
flood and the problems that comes with it, it rises above
it.

Ideation, art direction, branding, the full creative execution is mine, start to finish, and my team made it all
come to life. That's not a footnote here, it's the point: the difference between a campaign that gets ignored
and one that gets used at all was one person noticing a detail everyone else had walked past and refusing to let
it stay unexamined.

We tested that belief on July 4, 2026, at a barangay-wide event with 41 households. Good communication was

that evacuates in time and one that doesn't.
